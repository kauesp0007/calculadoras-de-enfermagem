Surgical Center

Equipment used within the Operating Room

Illustrations of equipment used within the operating room

1. C-Arm (Surgical Arc)

A C-Arm (or radioscopy) is performed using a mobile X-ray device known as the "C-Arm." It allows dynamic, real-time visualization of the patient's internal anatomy during surgery, being essential in orthopedic, urological, vascular surgeries, and pacemaker implants.

Nursing Role:

  • Ensure the use of radiological PPE (lead aprons and thyroid shields) for all team members in the room.
  • Assist in the safe positioning of the equipment without breaking the sterile field, often using sterile covers on the C-arm.
  • Ensure patient comfort and positioning, as the surgical table must be radiolucent and moved according to X-ray needs.

2. Infusion Pump

A infusion pump is a programmable electronic device designed to infuse fluids, nutrients, or medications in a controlled, precise, and safe manner into the patient's circulatory system. In the operating room, it is essential for continuous hydration, vasoactive drugs, antibiotics, and neuromuscular blockers.

Nursing Role:

  • Correctly assemble the administration set (specific to each pump) and prime it without air bubbles.
  • Program volume, time, and flow rate (ml/h) according to medical prescription or anesthetic protocol.
  • Continuously monitor visual and audible alarms (occlusion, air in line, end of infusion).

3. Syringe Pump

Similar to the standard infusion pump, but specifically designed to push the plunger of a syringe. The Syringe Pump is used for administering small volumes with millimeter precision, being the gold standard for total intravenous anesthesia (TIVA), infusion of strong opioids, and potent sedatives where error margins are not tolerated.

Nursing Role:

  • Correctly identify the attached syringe with the drug name, concentration, and preparation.
  • Ensure perfect fit of the syringe in the pump to avoid accidental bolus infusions (siphon effect).
  • Be alert for syringe replacement time, avoiding interruptions in sedation or analgesia during surgery.

4. Capnograph

O Capnograph is a non-invasive monitor that measures the concentration or partial pressure of carbon dioxide (CO2) in the patient's exhaled air (EtCO2), creating a continuous waveform (capnogram). It is the fastest and most reliable tool to confirm successful endotracheal intubation and to monitor pulmonary ventilation effectiveness in patients under general anesthesia.

Nursing Role:

  • Assist in connecting the capnography line between the ventilatory circuit (tube/laryngeal mask) and the monitor module.
  • Observe the waveform to early detect accidental extubation or obstructions (abnormal waves).
  • Check the integrity of the sampling line, ensuring no moisture or secretion blocks the sensor.

5. Cardioverter

O Cardioverter (often with built-in defibrillator function) is a vital emergency device. It delivers an electrical shock to the heart to reverse life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias. Synchronized cardioversion is used for tachyarrhythmias, while defibrillation (asynchronous shock) is the main intervention for Ventricular Fibrillation (cardiac arrest).

Nursing Role:

  • Test the device daily, ensuring it is always plugged in for full charge.
  • During use, prepare paddles with conductive gel or apply disposable adhesive pads quickly and correctly.
  • Ensure scene safety (clear everyone from the bed/metal table) at the moment of the "shock" commanded by the physician.

6. Emergency Cart

O Emergency Cart is a mobile structured cabinet with drawers containing everything needed for immediate response to cardiac arrest or severe complications. Its drawers are standardized, dividing medications, airway materials (laryngoscope, tubes), vascular access materials, and IV fluids/solutions.

Nursing Role:

  • Ensure all supplies are checked, verifying expiration dates and quantities according to institutional protocol.
  • Break the seal only in emergencies and ensure complete restocking immediately after the event.
  • Keep the cart in an easily accessible, unobstructed location, always ready for use in the operating room.

7. Anesthesia Machine

O Anesthesia Machine (or Anesthesia Station) is one of the most complex pieces of equipment in the operating room. It provides oxygen and precise mixing of medical gases and volatile anesthetics (vaporizers). Additionally, it functions as an integrated mechanical ventilator and has a CO2 absorption system (soda lime) for safe breathing under general anesthesia.

Nursing Role:

  • Correctly connect gases to the wall outlet and check the breathing circuit (sterile/disposable circuits) before each surgery.
  • Support the anesthesiologist during induction and reversal of anesthesia.
  • Check the saturation and expiration of soda lime (which changes color when exhausted) and replace it according to institutional guidelines.

8. Multiparameter Monitor

O Multiparameter Monitor captures, processes, and displays essential vital signs in real time. Basic parameters include electrocardiogram (ECG), heart rate, pulse oximetry (SpO2), non-invasive blood pressure (NIBP), and body temperature. In major surgeries, modules such as invasive blood pressure (IBP) are also connected.

Nursing Role:

  • Quickly and safely install sensors (chest electrodes, finger oximeter, and arm cuff) as soon as the patient arrives at the surgical table.
  • Pay attention to alarms during the procedure, informing the anesthesiologist of any abrupt changes.
  • Ensure cleaning and disinfection of cables and sensors between patients.

9. Surgical Table

A Surgical Table (or Operating Table) is the epicenter of the operating room. It is a robust, articulated, often motorized device designed to safely support the patient and allow positioning in various angles and complex positions (Trendelenburg, lithotomy, prone, Fowler) necessary for surgical access.

Nursing Role:

  • Operate the table controls under medical guidance, adjusting the correct anatomical position gradually.
  • Ensure patient protection by placing gel or foam pads on bony prominences to prevent pressure injuries and nerve damage.
  • Properly fasten safety straps and lock the table base to prevent any unwanted movement.

10. Mayo Stand

A Mayo Stand is an adjustable-height structure with an inverted "L" tray positioned directly over (cantilevered) the patient's lower limbs. On it, the scrub nurse organizes instruments for immediate and continuous use (scalpels, scissors, hemostatic forceps) following surgical steps (diérese, hemostasis, exeresis, and synthesis).

Nursing Role:

  • Drape the table with sterile covers or drapes (usually under the responsibility of the scrub nurse or nurse).
  • The circulating nurse must not touch the edges or surface of the table, supplying sterile materials by gently tossing them onto the field or delivering them using aseptic technique.
  • Avoid mechanical shocks and bumps to the table to prevent breaking the aseptic technique of the environment and to avoid dropping instruments.

11. Auxiliary Table

A Auxiliary Table (Back Table) is a rectangular and wider table, strategically positioned near the scrub nurse but away from the direct line of surgery. It serves to accommodate extra stock of gauze, sponges, suture threads, basins with solutions, heavy supplementary instrument trays, and sterile equipment that does not fit on the Mayo table.

Nursing Role:

  • The nurse or circulating technician assists the scrub nurse by opening packaging of supplies and sterile trays, offering them on the auxiliary table.
  • Collaborate in the rigorous counting of sponges and sharps that are on the auxiliary table before closing the patient's cavity.
  • Protect and mark the space of the auxiliary table so that non-sterile personnel do not contaminate the area.

12. Surgical Light

O Surgical Light is a high-power lighting structure positioned hanging from the ceiling of the room. It uses advanced halogen or LED technology to emit a cold light that does not dry out the patient's tissues, and has optical geometry designed to eliminate shadowing from the surgeon's head and hands on the operative field.

Nursing Role:

  • Attach autoclavable silicone handles (sterile) to the center of the light, allowing the sterile surgeon to adjust the light.
  • Turn on the equipment, adjust the satellite lights upon external request, and regulate the light intensity if the doctor requests.
  • Perform thorough concurrent cleaning after each use (on the non-sterile portion) to prevent dust accumulation over the operative area.

13. Video Laparoscopy Equipment

This equipment consists of a Video Laparoscopy Tower, a complex rack that houses: a high-definition surgical monitor, a camera processor, a powerful cold light source (LED or Xenon), and a CO2 insufflator, which generates pneumoperitoneum (abdominal inflation) to create internal space for instruments. It is the basis of modern minimally invasive surgery.

Nursing Role:

  • Install and check the pressures of the external Carbon Dioxide (CO2) cylinder connected to the insufflator.
  • Receive the sterile cables (fiber optic cable, gas hose) from the scrub nurse's hands and connect them safely to the tower.
  • Handle optics and cables with extreme care, as they are made of highly fragile and expensive crystal or fibers.

14. Surgical Aspirator

O Surgical Aspirator is a suction (vacuum) generator motor coupled to graduated collection canisters. Its purpose is to remove in real time extravasated blood, exudates, irrigation saline, tissue fragments, and electrocautery smoke from the surgical bed, providing the surgeon with a clean visual field to operate.

Nursing Role:

  • Connect the sterile hoses (coming from the surgical field) to the aspirator connector canister.
  • Monitor the filling of the collection canister, proceeding with immediate replacement if it reaches the maximum limit to avoid losing suction during critical moments.
  • Handle the aspirated fluid with maximum biosafety rigor (PPE), disposing of fluids in designated biohazard containers and/or solidifiers.

15. Nitrous Oxide Cylinder

O Nitrous Oxide Cylinder (N2O) is standardized by the color blue nationwide. This gas (often historically known as "laughing gas") is used by the anesthesiologist mixed with oxygen as an analgesic and hypnotic adjuvant, potentiating volatile inhaled anesthetics.

Nursing Role:

  • Act in the operating room checklist by attesting that the reserve cylinders are full via the pressure gauge.
  • Know how to identify gases by the universal safety color (Green=O2, Yellow=Compressed Air, Blue=Nitrous Oxide).
  • Ensure correct storage and immobilization of reserve cylinders in the room to prevent tipping and risk of accidents.
