Apgar Score

Assesses the newborn's physical condition (NB) immediately after birth.

The Apgar Score evaluates the newborn's physical condition (NB) at two crucial moments: 1 and 5 minutes of life. It assesses five criteria: heart rate, respiratory effort, muscle tone, reflex irritability, and skin color.

Year Created: 1952

Author: Dr. Virginia Apgar

Application Area: Delivery rooms and obstetric centers

Criteria Assessed: Heart Rate, Respiratory Effort, Muscle Tone, Reflex Irritability, and Skin Color

Used By: Pediatricians, neonatologists, and obstetric nurses

What is the Apgar Score?

The Apgar Score is a standardized assessment tool used worldwide to quickly evaluate a newborn's condition after delivery. Each criterion is scored from 0 to 2, with a maximum total score of 10 points.

Assessment Criteria:

  • Heart Rate: Assesses the newborn's pulse and cardiac activity
  • Respiratory Effort: Evaluates breathing pattern and quality
  • Muscle Tone: Examines limb movement and muscle strength
  • Reflex Irritability: Tests response to stimulation
  • Skin Color: Assesses oxygenation and circulation

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good Apgar score?

A score of 7-10 is considered normal and indicates a newborn in good condition. Scores between 4-6 suggest moderate distress, while scores below 4 indicate severe distress requiring immediate medical attention.

When is the Apgar score performed?

The Apgar score is traditionally performed at 1 minute and 5 minutes after birth. Additional assessments may be done at 10, 15, and 20 minutes if the initial scores are low.

Who performs the Apgar assessment?

The Apgar score is typically performed by a pediatrician, neonatologist, or obstetric nurse immediately after delivery.

Can the Apgar score predict long-term outcomes?

While the Apgar score is an important immediate assessment tool, it should not be used alone to predict long-term development or neurological outcomes. It is part of a comprehensive newborn evaluation.
